In his 2025 year-in-review, Andrej Karpathy argued that "vibe coding" had crossed a threshold: programming was no longer a specialized skill but something anyone who speaks natural language can do. The shift wasn't about better autocomplete — it was about AI understanding intent well enough that describing what you want became sufficient to build it. The professional programmer, Karpathy wrote, was no longer the gatekeeper of software creation.

The implications extend beyond individual productivity. When the barrier to creating software drops to zero, the bottleneck moves from "who can code" to "who has the idea." Organizations that previously needed developers to translate every request into code can now route those requests directly to AI — exactly the model that tools like Claude Channels are built around. Karpathy's observation frames the broader trend: accessibility isn't a feature of AI tools, it's the entire point.
